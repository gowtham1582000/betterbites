1. Kolkata's St. Xavier's Collegiate School

St. Xavier's Collegiate is a private, English-medium school for boys and is regarded as one of the best in India. In 1860, the Jesuits founded it. The Council for the Indian School Certificate Examinations (CISCE), which administers the ISCE and ISC exams in classes X and XII, is linked with St. Xavier's Collegiate School. Also, it provides the curriculum used by the West Bengal Board of Secondary Education, which administers the Madhyamik exams following class X. For secondary and higher secondary exams up to class X, students may select one of the two curricula. Courses for the ICSE and ISC boards begin in classes XI and XII because fewer pupils choose the West Bengal board. The school hosts extracurricular events like debate, quizzes, scouts and guides, photography exhibitions, film screenings and sports like badminton, table tennis, football, cricket and track and field. The school also features a social service committee, a wildlife club, and a computer.

Historical Development of Education in India

The Gurukul was India's original educational system. It was a form of residential education that dates back to circa 5000 BC and required both the learner and the teacher to live in or close to the guru's ashram. This made it possible for an emotional connection to be made before knowledge was shared. The ancient Sanskrit language served as the primary language of exchange.
Images by @tim_arterbury

Learning was not just about memorising facts and reading books; it also took into account a child's overall growth. This covers their spiritual, physical, mental, and cognitive health. Religion, sacred texts, medicine, philosophy, warfare, statecraft, astrology, and other topics were covered in the classroom. The focus was on fostering in the students strong moral and ethical behaviours as well as human characteristics like independence, empathy, and creativity. The idea was that this information may be applied later on in order to solve practical issues.

The Gurukul's educational objectives were as follows:

The pursuit of ultimate knowledge: The Gurukul educational system's main goal was to help students understand Brahma (God) and the world beyond sensory delights in order to achieve immortality. Character development: By studying the Vedas (old books), the learner gained the willpower necessary for excellent character, which in turn enabled them to acquire a more favourable attitude and optimistic outlook on life. wholesome development : It was believed that practising introversion and learning to shut off the outside world was the best way to live a full life. When they carried out various tasks in the Gurukul, this enabled the students to become aware of the inner workings of the mind as well as their responses and reactions.

The education system in india today

Unquestionably, education in contemporary India has advanced from that of the "Gurukula." The emphasis is mainly on the competitive test and grades than than moral, ethical, and spiritual education, and the curriculum is primarily taught in English or Hindi. Computer technology and skills have also been integrated into learning systems.

Lord Thomas Babington Macaulay first introduced the contemporary educational system to India in the 1830s. Science, mathematics, and other "modern" topics were prioritised, whereas metaphysics and philosophy were seen as superfluous.

Because of the new National Education Policy (NEP), the 5+3+3+4 system has taken its place. The classification of stages has been developed to correspond with the stages of cognitive growth that a kid inevitably experiences.
